## Common Meditation Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Yellow practitioners are especially susceptible to certain meditation traps. Here's how to navigate them.

**Pitfall 1: Technique Hopping**

You read about a new meditation method. It sounds perfect. You switch. Two weeks later, you read about another one. You switch again. Six months in, you've tried fifteen techniques but mastered none.

This is Yellow's shadow: the addiction to novelty, the belief that the next technique will be the one that finally "works."

**The Fix:** Pick one technique and stick with it for at least six months. Depth beats breadth. You're not looking for the perfect method—you're developing the capacity to work with *any* method deeply.

**Pitfall 2: Spiritual Materialism**

You start meditating to "get somewhere." To achieve jhāna. To have mystical experiences. To become enlightened. The meditation becomes another achievement project.

This is Orange's shadow creeping into Yellow. You're turning a practice of presence into a performance metric.

**The Fix:** Let go of outcomes. Meditate to meditate. Not to get calm, not to have insights, not to prove anything. Just to be here. The paradox: the less you chase states, the more naturally they arise.

**Pitfall 3: Using Meditation to Bypass Therapy**

You've got unprocessed trauma. Deep wounds. And you think meditation will heal them. So you sit through flashbacks, dissociation, panic—believing you're "working through it."

But meditation is not therapy. If you're dealing with significant trauma, you need *both*—meditation for awareness, therapy for processing.

**The Fix:** If meditation consistently destabilizes you (panic attacks, dissociation, suicidal ideation), pause. Get professional support. Heal the trauma. Then return to the cushion. There's no heroism in re-traumatizing yourself.

**Pitfall 4: Becoming a "Meditator" as Identity**

You sit every day. You're proud of it. You start to see yourself as more evolved than people who don't meditate. You subtly judge them. You use meditation as a status marker.

This is Green's shadow: spiritual ego. You've made the practice about being special.

**The Fix:** Hold your practice lightly. Meditate because it's useful, not because it makes you superior. And recognize: the best meditators are often the most humble. They know how little they know.

**Pitfall 5: Confusing States with Stages**

You have a profound sit. Everything dissolves. You touch something vast. You think, "I've arrived. I'm enlightened."

Then the next day, you're anxious and irritable. And you're confused. "What happened?"

What happened: you confused a temporary state with a permanent stage. States come and go. Stages are developmental—they stick.

**The Fix:** Don't chase peak experiences. Don't cling to bliss. Notice the state, appreciate it, let it pass. Keep training. Stages emerge from consistent practice over years, not from one good sit.
