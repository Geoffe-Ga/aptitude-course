## "INTEGRATE (Do)"—Full 6-Phase Wavelength Breakdown

Yellow's Wavelength is the rhythm of focused attention. It's the map of how the mind trains itself—how concentration builds, stabilizes, falters, and renews. Each phase has its medicine and its poison.

### 1. RISING

**Rx: Focus | OD: Hyperfocus**

Rising in Yellow is about gathering your attention. You sit down to meditate, open a book, start a project—and you direct your mind toward a single object. In its medicine form, this is **Focus**—the capacity to bring your awareness to what matters and sustain it there.

Focus is steady but relaxed. You're not forcing. You're *inviting* your attention to rest on the breath, the page, the task. And when it wanders, you gently guide it back.

This is the skill of voluntary attention. And it's trainable. Every time you notice distraction and return to the object, you're strengthening the neural pathways of focus. You're building the muscle.

Focus is what allows you to read a dense text and actually comprehend it. To sit in meditation without getting lost in thought spirals. To have a conversation without checking your phone. It's the foundation of all higher cognition.

But when Focus becomes rigid, it tips into **Hyperfocus**.

Hyperfocus is when you lock onto something so intensely that you lose peripheral awareness. You're *grinding*. You're so fixated on the object that you miss everything else—your body screaming for rest, the time passing, the people trying to get your attention.

You see this in the person who gets so absorbed in work that they skip meals, forget to sleep, burn out. In the meditator who's so determined to "nail the technique" that they lose the joy of practice. In the student who's so focused on getting an A that they don't actually *learn* anything.

Hyperfocus is concentration without balance. It's the tyranny of a single point.

The antidote is what Culadasa calls "introspective awareness"—the part of your mind that watches the focusing. You're not just concentrated *on* the breath; you're also *aware* that you're concentrating. And that awareness keeps the focus flexible, responsive, alive.

When you notice Hyperfocus creeping in—when your body is tense, your jaw clenched, your breath shallow—pause. Soften. Widen your awareness. Let the focus stay, but let it breathe.

Focus is medicine. Hyperfocus is poison. Both live in Rising. Your work is to stay focused without rigidity.

### 2. PEAKING

**Rx: Concentration | OD: Obsession**

Peaking is when focus deepens into **Concentration**—what the contemplative traditions call *samadhi*. This is when the mind unifies. When distractions fade. When you're so absorbed in the object that subject-object duality begins to dissolve.

Concentration is effortless focus. You're not working to stay with the breath—you're *resting* in it. The mind has stopped fragmenting. It's become smooth, stable, luminous. This is where pīti (joy) and sukha (bliss) arise naturally. Not because you're forcing them, but because the mind at rest *is* blissful.

This is the state meditators train for. The flow state athletes talk about. The zone musicians enter. It's when performance becomes effortless because the doer has dissolved into the doing.

Concentration is also the state where insight becomes possible. Because when the mind is stable, it can *see* clearly. It can observe the arising and passing of phenomena without getting caught in them. It can glimpse the nature of consciousness itself.

But Concentration has a shadow. When you become *attached* to this state—when you chase it, grasp at it, make it your identity—it becomes **Obsession**.

Obsession is the addiction to peak states. It's the meditator who sits for hours chasing the bliss they felt once and can't replicate. It's the flow-state junkie who can't function unless they're "in the zone." It's the spiritual seeker who mistakes altered states for enlightenment.

This is a seductive trap. Because Concentration *does* feel amazing. It's peaceful, expansive, deeply satisfying. And the ego loves it. It wants to claim this state as proof of progress, as evidence of specialness.

But here's the truth: states come and go. Concentration arises and passes. If you cling to it, you suffer. If you chase it, you lose it.

The medicine is equanimity. You appreciate Concentration when it arises. You don't grasp when it fades. You recognize it as one phase of the cycle, not the destination.

Culadasa talks about this in *The Mind Illuminated*: "Don't confuse states with stages. States are temporary. Stages are developmental." Concentration is a state. Integration is a stage. Don't mistake the high for the goal.

When you notice Obsession—when you're chasing the feeling, frustrated that the meditation isn't "working," attached to a particular experience—let go. Return to the simple practice. Trust the process.

Concentration is medicine. Obsession is poison. Both live in Peaking. Choose presence over attachment.

### 3. WITHDRAWAL

**Rx: Adjustment | OD: Stagnation**

Every peak passes. The concentration falters. The mind starts to wander again. This is Withdrawal—the natural ebb of attention. And in its medicine form, it's **Adjustment**—the capacity to notice when you've drifted and gently course-correct.

Adjustment is not dramatic. It's subtle. You're meditating and you realize you've been lost in thought for a minute. Instead of berating yourself, you simply note it—"thinking"—and return to the breath. That's Adjustment.

Or you're working on a project and you notice you've been procrastinating. Instead of spiraling into shame, you close the distracting tab and refocus. That's Adjustment.

Adjustment is the wisdom of the feedback loop. You notice the deviation. You correct. You continue. No drama. No self-flagellation. Just responsive course-correction.

This is what makes mastery sustainable. You don't expect yourself to maintain peak focus forever. You *expect* to drift—and you've trained the skill of returning.

But when Adjustment is avoided, Withdrawal becomes **Stagnation**.

Stagnation is when you notice you've drifted… and you just stay there. You know you're off course, but you don't do anything about it. You've lost the will to correct.

In meditation, this looks like: "I'm distracted. Oh well. Guess I'll just sit here thinking for the next 30 minutes." You've given up on the practice without actually quitting.

In life, it looks like: "I know this relationship/job/habit isn't working. But changing feels too hard. So I'll just… stay stuck." You've noticed the problem but refused the solution.

Stagnation is the death of growth. It's awareness without action. And it's Yellow's specific trap—because Yellow *sees* so clearly that it can become paralyzed by what it sees.

The antidote is small action. You don't need to fix everything. You just need to take *one step* back toward alignment. Adjust. Even if it's imperfect. Even if you drift again in five minutes. The practice is the returning.

When you notice Stagnation—when you're aware you're off track but doing nothing—make one micro-adjustment. Return to the breath. Send the text. Close the tab. Take the smallest possible step.

Adjustment is medicine. Stagnation is poison. Both live in Withdrawal. Choose movement.

### 4. DIMINISHING

**Rx: Reorienting | OD: Dithering**

The energy continues to drop. The initial clarity is fading. You're not sure what you're doing anymore or why. This is Diminishing. And in its medicine form, it's **Reorienting**—the practice of stepping back to reassess.

Reorienting is strategic pause. You stop trying to push through and instead ask: What am I actually trying to do here? Is this still the right approach? What needs to shift?

In meditation, this might look like: "I've been straining to focus on the breath for 20 minutes and it's not working. Maybe I need to switch to body scanning for a bit." You're not abandoning the practice—you're *adjusting the method*.

In a project, it looks like: "This strategy isn't working. Let me zoom out, look at the bigger picture, and recalibrate." You're not quitting—you're being strategic about the path forward.

Reorienting is the wisdom of flexibility. You hold your goals lightly enough to change tactics when the situation calls for it. You're committed to the outcome, not the method.

But when Reorienting becomes compulsive, it tips into **Dithering**.

Dithering is endlessly changing course without ever committing. You try one thing, get uncertain, switch to another. Try that, get uncertain, switch again. You're constantly reassessing but never actually *doing*.

In meditation, this is the person who can't stick with one technique. Breath meditation one day, mantra the next, body scan the day after. They're always "optimizing," but they never build depth.

In life, it's the person who starts a new project every month but finishes none. Who reads every self-help book but implements nothing. Who's always "figuring out their next move" but never moves.

Dithering is paralysis disguised as discernment. It's the Yellow shadow in full bloom—so aware of all the options that you can't commit to any.

The antidote is: *pick something and stick with it*. Even if it's not perfect. Even if another option might be slightly better. Commit. Give it time. Trust that depth beats breadth.

When you notice Dithering—when you're endlessly tweaking, researching, re-strategizing without actually executing—make a decision. Commit to it for a defined period (a week, a month, a season). Then honor that commitment.

Reorienting is medicine. Dithering is poison. Both live in Diminishing. Choose commitment.

### 5. BOTTOMING OUT

**Rx: Settling | OD: Depression**

This is the nadir. The place where all the forward momentum has drained away and you're left in the stillness. In its medicine form, this is **Settling**—the deep rest that comes from complete release of striving.

Settling is when you stop trying to make anything happen. You're not forcing focus. You're not chasing concentration. You're just… here. Sitting. Breathing. Being.

This is the wisdom of non-doing. The recognition that sometimes the most productive thing you can do is nothing. Just let the system reset.

In meditation, Settling is when you've abandoned all technique and you're just resting in awareness. Not effortful. Not goal-oriented. Just present.

In life, it's when you finally admit you're exhausted and you *rest*. No agenda. No hustle. Just allowing the body and mind to recover.

Settling is trust. Trust that you don't have to be "on" all the time. That the Wavelength will turn upward again. That rest is not failure—it's part of the cycle.

But when Settling tips into hopelessness, it becomes **Depression**.

Depression is the belief that the low point is permanent. That nothing will ever feel good again. That effort is pointless. You're not resting—you're *giving up*.

This is the dark night. The place where motivation dies. Where the practice feels meaningless and life feels empty and you can't remember why you ever thought any of this mattered.

Depression is Settling without trust. It's the bottom without the faith that there's another side.

And here's the thing: Depression is *real*. It's not just "being in a low phase." If you're experiencing clinical depression, *get help*. Talk to a therapist. Consider medication. Don't try to meditate your way out of a neurochemical crisis.

But for the existential kind of depression—the kind that arises when you've seen through all your previous sources of meaning and haven't yet found new ones—the medicine is *continuing the practice anyway*.

Sit even when it feels pointless. Meditate even when the mind is chaos. Show up even when you don't believe it matters. Because the practice is what carries you through. And on the other side of the depression, you'll find that the faith you lost is waiting for you—deeper, more grounded, more real.

The Self-Care Strategies for Bottoming Out include readjusting posture, drinking water, listening to music, gentle breathwork, and *reaching out for support*. Don't try to white-knuckle it alone.

Settling is medicine. Depression is poison. Both live in Bottoming Out. Choose trust.

### 6. RESTORATION

**Rx: Reset | OD: Addiction**

The energy begins to return. You're coming back online. And at Restoration, Yellow asks: How will you re-engage?

In its medicine form, this is **Reset**—the practice of beginning again, fresh, without carrying the baggage of the last cycle.

Reset is the wisdom of impermanence. You don't cling to what worked last time. You don't avoid what failed. You just… start again. Clean slate. Beginner's mind.

In meditation, Reset is when you finish a difficult sit—distracted, restless, frustrated—and the next day you come back to the cushion as if it's your first time. No story. No expectation. Just this breath.

In life, it's when you finish a hard project, take a beat, and then ask: "What's next?" without needing the next thing to redeem the last.

Reset is resilience. It's the capacity to metabolize failure and success with equal grace and then *keep going*.

But when Reset becomes compulsive, it tips into **Addiction**.

Addiction at Yellow is not just substances. It's the addiction to *novelty*. To the dopamine hit of starting something new. To the fantasy that the next book, the next training, the next technique will be the one that finally fixes you.

You see this in the person who's always "just starting" a new meditation practice. Who buys every course but finishes none. Who chases the high of Restoration but never stabilizes in the practice.

Addiction is Reset without integration. It's the refusal to actually *be* with what you've learned. You're so eager to move on to the next thing that you never digest the last.

The antidote is *depth*. Instead of resetting to something new, reset *into* what you've already committed to. Go deeper. Explore the nuances. Let the practice mature.

When you notice Addiction—when you're craving the next shiny thing, when you're bored with what you're doing even though you've barely scratched the surface—pause. Recommit. Give yourself permission to be a beginner *in this practice* instead of always being a beginner in new ones.

Reset is medicine. Addiction is poison. Both live in Restoration. Choose depth.
