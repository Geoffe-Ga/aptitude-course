## The Odyssey: Integration as Homecoming

Let me tell you about the original story of integration. It's Greek. Three thousand years old. And it maps Yellow's journey with startling precision.

Odysseus spent ten years fighting the Trojan War. Then another ten years trying to get home. Twenty years of wandering, of facing monsters and gods and his own hubris, before he finally returned to Ithaca.

But here's what most people miss about *The Odyssey*: it's not just a story about a journey home. It's a story about *who you become* through the journey. About integration.

When Odysseus finally arrives in Ithaca, he doesn't announce himself. He disguises himself as a beggar. He observes. He tests. Because he knows: he's not the same man who left. And Ithaca is not the same place.

The suitors have overrun his home. His son has grown up without him. His wife has aged. Everything has changed.

And Odysseus—who's faced Cyclops and Sirens and the underworld itself—has to navigate this new reality with all the skills he's acquired. He can't just dominate (that's Red). He can't just follow the old rules (that's Blue). He has to *integrate* everything he's learned and act from that wholeness.

This is Yellow's template.
