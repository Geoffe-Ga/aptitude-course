## "INTEGRATE (Feel)"—Full 6-Phase Wavelength Breakdown

Teal's Wavelength is the same structure as Yellow's, but the *quality* is different. Where Yellow was effortful, Teal is effortless. Where Yellow was directed, Teal is receptive. Let's walk through how each phase *feels* at Teal.

### 1. RISING

**Rx: Focus | OD: Hyperfocus**

At Yellow, Focus was something you *did*. At Teal, Focus is something that *happens*.

You don't force your attention onto the breath. You just… notice that attention has gathered there. Like a bird landing on a branch. You didn't make it land. It just chose to.

This is the shift from doing to allowing. You're still present with the object of meditation (the breath, the body, the field), but you're not gripping it. You're *with* it.

**What This Feels Like in Practice:**

You sit down for your walk. The first few minutes, your mind is scattered—lists of things to do, snippets of conversations, worries about the future. This is normal. At Yellow, you would *corral* the attention, firmly redirecting it back to the present. At Teal, you just... notice the scattering. You don't fight it.

And then—without forcing it—your attention begins to gather. Maybe a bird calls and pulls you into sound. Maybe the rhythm of your footsteps becomes hypnotic. Maybe the quality of light filtering through leaves catches you.

The key word is "catches." You don't *grab* the present moment. It grabs you.

This is effortless focus. It's what happens when you stop trying to meditate and just let meditation happen. The attention *wants* to settle. You just have to stop interfering with its natural movement.

**Recognizing and Releasing Hyperfocus:**

But sometimes—especially for those of us with neurodivergent brains prone to hyperfocus—the attention locks on too hard. You're so absorbed in watching your breath that you're barely breathing. You're so fixated on the sensation in your foot that your whole body tenses.

Hyperfocus feels like intensity. It has a quality of strain, even when you're "doing it right." There's a subtle aggression in it—a need to *capture* the object and pin it down.

When Hyperfocus arises at Teal, it feels like tightness. A subtle constriction. You notice it immediately because you've trained the capacity to sense when effort is creeping in. And you soften. You don't force the softening—you just *allow* the grip to release.

How? By widening your attention. If you're hyperfocused on the breath, expand to include the whole body. If you're hyperfocused on the body, expand to include sounds. If you're hyperfocused on any particular object, expand to include the *space* in which all objects appear.

This is the self-correcting mechanism of Teal: when focus gets too tight, you soften into spaciousness. And the paradox is that spacious awareness can hold more detail, more nuance, more aliveness than narrow focus ever could.

This is the medicine of Teal's Rising: effortless attention. Not because you've stopped caring, but because you've learned to trust.

**Off the Cushion: Effortless Focus in Daily Life**

You're in a conversation. At Orange, you'd be actively listening—tracking what they're saying, formulating your response, managing your body language. That's skillful, but it's effortful.

At Teal, you just... listen. You're not trying to be a good listener. You're just *here*. And somehow, you hear more. You catch the nuance in their tone, the pause before they answer, the thing they're not saying. Because you're not working so hard at listening, you're actually present for it.

Or you're writing. At Yellow, you'd be disciplining yourself to stay on task, resisting distractions, pushing through resistance. At Teal, you just sit down and... words come. Or they don't. And if they don't, you wait. You trust that when the words are ready, they'll arrive.

This isn't passivity. It's a different kind of engagement. You're showing up, opening the space, and letting the thing happen through you rather than forcing it to happen *by* you.

When you've mastered Rising at Teal, focus becomes play. It's light, curious, responsive. You're not grimly concentrating. You're delighted by what's arising, moment by moment. And that delight is what sustains the attention—not willpower, but genuine interest in what's alive right now.

### 2. PEAKING

**Rx: Concentration | OD: Obsession**

At Teal, Concentration is not something you achieve. It's something you *recognize*.

The mind unifies. The sense of separate self softens. You're still here, but the boundaries have become porous. You're not "you watching the breath"—you're just *breathing*.

This is where bliss arises naturally. But at Teal, you don't grasp at it. You let it come. You let it go. Like a wave passing through.

**The Phenomenology of Unified Attention:**

You're 30 minutes into your walk. The scattered thoughts have quieted. The commentary has stopped. And suddenly—though there's no "suddenly," it happened so gradually you didn't notice—you're just... here.

Not "you being here." Just *hereness*.

The trees aren't separate from you. The path isn't separate from the walking. The breathing isn't something you're doing—it's something happening in the same field as the bird call, the breeze, the warmth of sun on your skin.

This is concentration at Teal. Not because you've narrowed your attention to a single point, but because everything has become one field. There's no subject watching an object. There's just experiencing.

The Zen tradition calls this *shikantaza*—"just sitting" or in our case, "just walking." You're not concentrating *on* anything. You're resting *as* the concentrated state itself.

And it feels... effortless. Easeful. Like you've come home to something you never really left.

**When Bliss Arises—and How Not to Grasp It:**

Sometimes, in this unified state, bliss arises. It can be subtle—a gentle warmth spreading through your chest, a softening in your face, a felt sense of *rightness*. Or it can be intense—waves of euphoria, tears of gratitude, a body-wide sensation of love pouring through you.

At Yellow, you'd be tempted to analyze it: "What caused this? How do I replicate it? This must mean I'm doing it right."

At Teal, you just... let it be. You don't grab for it. You don't try to make it stay. You don't even label it as "bliss." You just feel it fully, completely, without adding story.

And here's what's wild: when you don't grasp, the bliss *deepens*. It's not that the sensation intensifies necessarily—it's that your capacity to receive it expands. You're not constricting around it. You're letting it move through you like water through an open hand.

This is the medicine of Peaking at Teal: you can experience profound states without *identifying* with them. They're weather patterns in consciousness. Beautiful. Real. And temporary.

**Obsession at Teal: The Subtle Grasp**

Obsession at Teal is more subtle than at Yellow. It's not gross clinging—"I need this state to last forever!" It's a whisper of preference. A tiny hope that the state will continue. And as soon as you notice it, you see how it creates contraction. And you let it go.

It looks like this: you're in the unified state. Bliss is present. And then a thought arises: "This is it. This is what I've been working toward. If I can just stay here..."

And immediately, the state begins to fragment. The subject-object split reappears. There's "you" trying to maintain "the state." The unity dissolves.

This is obsession. Not dramatic, just... a subtle grasping that breaks the spell.

The antidote is noticing the grasping the moment it arises—and smiling at it. Not judging it, not pushing it away, just recognizing: "Ah, wanting. Hello, wanting." And in that recognition, the wanting releases. The unity returns.

Over time, you get faster at catching the grasp. Eventually, the grasping arises and releases so quickly that it barely disturbs the surface. You're learning to let even the desire for the state to continue just... pass through.

**The Teaching of Impermanence:**

Peaking at Teal teaches you viscerally what Buddhism has been saying all along: *anicca*—everything changes. Even the most profound states of consciousness are temporary. They arise, they peak, they pass. Always.

This isn't a problem. This isn't failure. This is just how reality works.

And when you truly understand this—not intellectually, but *experientially*—you stop chasing peak states. You stop measuring your practice by whether you "got somewhere." You're just showing up, being present with whatever phase you're in, trusting the cycle.

Peaking at Teal is *light*. You're concentrated, but not rigid. Absorbed, but not lost. Blissful, but not attached.

You're learning to hold the most beautiful experiences of your life *lightly*. Like a feather in an open palm. Not crushing it. Not letting it blow away. Just... holding it. For as long as it's here.

**Off the Cushion: Peaking Without Grasping**

You're in a moment of genuine connection—laughing with friends, making love, creating something beautiful, witnessing a sunset. The old pattern would be to think, "I wish this would never end. How do I make this last?"

At Teal, you don't think that. You just... drink it in. Fully. Completely. Knowing that this moment is already dissolving even as you're in it. And somehow, that knowledge makes it *more* precious, not less.

You're not trying to freeze the moment in memory or capture it in a photo (though you might take a photo—the difference is in the grasping). You're just *here*. And when the moment ends, you let it end. You don't mourn its passing. You just turn toward the next moment with the same quality of presence.

This is what it means to peak without obsessing. To experience the fullness of life without needing to own it.

### 3. WITHDRAWAL

**Rx: Adjustment | OD: Stagnation**

At Yellow, Adjustment was deliberate. At Teal, it's spontaneous.

The attention drifts. You don't scold yourself or force it back. You just… notice. And in the noticing, it returns. Not because you made it return, but because noticing *is* returning.

This is the wisdom of Teal: awareness itself is corrective. You don't have to *do* anything. You just have to *see*.

**The Natural Drift:**

You've been walking for 40 minutes. You were in a beautiful flow—unified, present, absorbed. And then... you're not. A thought pulls you in. A planning spiral starts. Or you just space out—daydreaming, drifting, gone.

Earlier in your practice, this would trigger self-judgment: "I lost it. I failed. I need to concentrate harder."

At Teal, it's just: "Oh. Drifting happened." No drama. No story. Just a neutral observation.

And here's the magic: the moment you notice the drift, you're already back. The noticing *itself* is presence. You don't need to do anything else.

It's like waking from a dream. You don't have to try to wake up—you just... are awake. And in that waking, you're here again.

This is spontaneous adjustment. The system self-corrects the moment awareness shines on it.

**The Somatic Feel of Adjustment:**

Adjustment at Teal has a particular body sensation. It's like a gentle recalibration—not a lurch or a jolt, but a soft settling back into alignment.

Maybe your shoulders drop. Maybe your breath deepens. Maybe your eyes soften. There's a felt sense of "oh yes, *this*." Like tuning a radio and suddenly the static clears and you can hear the music again.

Pay attention to this feeling. Because over time, you can start to recognize when you're drifting *before* you've fully left. You'll feel the pre-drift—a slight haziness, a subtle loss of sharpness—and you'll adjust before you're fully gone.

This is advanced practice. You're not waiting to fall asleep and then waking up. You're catching the moment when your eyelids start to droop and gently, almost imperceptibly, freshening your attention.

**Stagnation: The False Refuge of "Acceptance":**

Stagnation at Teal happens when you get lazy. When you notice the drift and think, "Eh, it's fine. I'm just being with what is." But really, you're *avoiding* the practice. You're mistaking passivity for acceptance.

Here's the difference:

**Acceptance:** You notice the drift. You see that the mind is scattered. You're fully aware that you're not in the unified state. And you're okay with that—not as an excuse to check out, but as a recognition that this is what's arising right now. From that place of acceptance, you gently return to presence. You're not forcing. But you're also not abandoning the practice.

**Passivity (Stagnation):** You notice the drift. And you use "acceptance" as permission to stop trying. You tell yourself, "I'm trusting the process," but really you're just checked out. You're still sitting (or walking), but you're not actually practicing. You're just... there. Going through the motions.

The difference is subtle. Acceptance is engaged. Passivity is checked out. You feel it in the body—acceptance has a quality of presence, even when the mind is messy. Passivity feels flat. Numb. Disengaged.

**How to Recognize Stagnation:**

Ask yourself: Am I *with* the drift, or am I lost *in* it?

If you're *with* it, you're aware. You're watching the mind wander. You're seeing the thoughts arise and pass. You're present for the distraction. That's acceptance.

If you're lost *in* it, you've forgotten you're practicing. You're fully identified with the story the mind is telling. There's no witness. That's stagnation.

When you notice Stagnation, you gently re-engage. Not with force. Just with a soft return to presence.

And you forgive yourself. Because stagnation isn't failure—it's just a phase. It happens. The practice is noticing it and choosing to return. Again. And again. And again.

**Off the Cushion: The Art of Course Correction:**

You're in a relationship and you notice you've drifted. You're going through the motions—saying "I love you" out of habit, not presence. Having conversations on autopilot. You're not *fighting*, but you're not really *connecting* either.

At Teal, this is just... withdrawal. A natural phase. Not a crisis. Not evidence that the relationship is doomed. Just a signal that it's time to adjust.

So you do. You initiate a real conversation. You make eye contact. You touch with intention. You ask a question you're genuinely curious about. Small adjustments. Gentle returns.

And suddenly—or gradually—you're back. Present. Connected. The drift was just a drift.

This is Teal's wisdom applied to life: everything drifts. Energy wanes. Attention scatters. Connection fades. That's not a problem. That's just the rhythm. The work is noticing when you've drifted and choosing to return—without judgment, without drama, just a soft adjustment back to presence.

### 4. DIMINISHING

**Rx: Reorienting | OD: Dithering**

At Teal, Reorienting is not strategic (like at Yellow). It's intuitive. You feel when something needs to shift. And you follow that feeling.

Maybe the breath practice isn't landing today. So you shift to body awareness. Or open awareness. You're not planning it—you're *sensing* it.

**Intuitive Reorientation:**

You're 45 minutes into your walk. The unified state has passed. The drift has come and gone. And now there's just... flatness. The practice feels dry. Mechanical. Like you're going through the motions but nothing's happening.

At Yellow, you'd push through. You'd double down on the technique. "Focus harder. Concentrate more."

At Teal, you listen to the dryness. You ask: What does this need? And you wait for the body's answer.

Maybe the answer is: shift from walking to sitting. Find a bench, close your eyes, drop into stillness.

Maybe it's: stop trying to meditate at all. Just walk. Let your mind wander. Give it permission to be messy.

Maybe it's: change your route. Take a different path. Let novelty wake you up.

You're not following a script. You're improvising based on what's alive in this moment. This is the art of Teal—trusting your felt sense more than your plan.

**The Difference Between Intuition and Preference:**

But here's the tricky part: how do you know if you're following intuition or just chasing comfort?

Intuition feels like a gentle pull. It's soft but persistent. It doesn't have urgency—it just has clarity. "This wants to shift."

Preference feels like agitation. It's driven by aversion—you're moving *away* from discomfort rather than *toward* what's needed. "I don't like this. I need it to be different. Now."

Intuition is responsive. Preference is reactive.

Here's a test: when you feel the impulse to change something in your practice, pause. Take three breaths. And ask: Am I running from something, or am I being called toward something?

If you're running—if there's a contracted, anxious quality to the impulse—that's dithering. Stay. Sit with the discomfort a little longer. See what it's teaching.

If you're being called—if there's an open, curious quality to the impulse—that's reorienting. Trust it. Make the shift.

**Dithering: The Restless Mind Dressed as Flexibility:**

Dithering at Teal looks like constantly changing practices because you can't tolerate discomfort. You're not listening to intuition—you're running from difficulty.

It's the practitioner who can't stick with one technique long enough to go deep. Who's always chasing the next practice, the next teacher, the next method. Who mistakes novelty for progress.

Dithering says: "This practice isn't working. Let me try something else." Over and over. Never staying long enough to see what the practice is actually offering.

The irony is that dithering often masquerades as open-mindedness. "I'm not attached to one method! I'm experimenting!" But underneath, it's just restlessness. An inability to be with the boring, unglamorous work of showing up to the same practice day after day, even when it's not giving you fireworks.

The medicine is trust. Sometimes the practice is supposed to be hard. Sometimes you're supposed to stay with the dryness, the boredom, the resistance. Not forever. But long enough to see what it's teaching.

Because here's what the seasoned practitioners know: the breakthrough often comes *right after* you've decided the practice isn't working. You've been sitting with dryness for weeks. And then, one day, for no apparent reason, everything shifts. The dryness was necessary. It was composting something. Preparing the ground.

If you dither—if you abandon the practice the moment it gets hard—you'll never see that shift.

**When to Stay, When to Shift:**

So how do you know?

**Stay if:**
- The difficulty has a quality of ripening. You can feel something working beneath the surface, even if it's not pleasant.
- You're bored, but not depleted. Boredom is often just the mind's resistance to simplicity. Push through it.
- You've been with this practice for less than a month. Give it time to work.

**Shift if:**
- The practice is causing harm. If it's triggering trauma, destabilizing your nervous system, or making you less functional, stop. Find support. Choose something gentler.
- You've been with the practice for months and there's genuinely no juice. Not just "no fireworks," but no subtle shifts either. Sometimes a practice has given you what it has to give, and it's time to move on.
- Your intuition is clear and persistent. Not a fleeting whim, but a deep knowing: this isn't my path. Trust that.

Reorienting is responsive. Dithering is reactive. Feel the difference.

**Off the Cushion: Knowing When to Pivot:**

You're in a job, a relationship, a city. It's not terrible. But it's not alive either. You're going through the motions. And you wonder: Do I stay and work with this? Or do I leave?

At Teal, you apply the same discernment. Is this dryness part of the process—something to be metabolized, worked through, integrated? Or is this a genuine call to reorient?

You sit with the question. You feel into it. You notice: is there a quality of ripening here? Am I learning something? Growing in some subtle way?

Or is this just... dead? Am I staying out of fear, out of inertia, out of a story that I "should" make this work?

There's no formula. But at Teal, you trust your felt sense. You let the body tell you. And when the answer is clear—not impulsive, but clear—you act.

Sometimes that means staying. Recommitting. Finding a new angle on the same situation.

Sometimes that means leaving. Trusting that the next chapter is calling and it's time to turn the page.

This is the wisdom of Diminishing: knowing when to hold on and when to let go. And having the courage to do either.

### 5. BOTTOMING OUT

**Rx: Settling | OD: Depression**

At Teal, Settling is not resignation. It's *rest*. Deep, complete, undefended rest.

You've stopped trying. Not because you've given up, but because you've realized that there's nowhere to get to. You're already here. And here is enough.

This is the wisdom of "just sitting" in Zen. Shikantaza. You're not meditating *to* achieve something. You're meditating because meditation is what's happening right now.

Depression at Teal is when Settling tips into nihilism. When "nowhere to get to" becomes "nothing matters." When rest becomes collapse.

The difference is aliveness. Settling is alive. Depression is numb. Settling includes sadness, but it's not crushed by it. Depression can't feel anything at all.

If you're in Depression, reach out. Get support. This is not a stage to push through alone.

### 6. RESTORATION

**Rx: Reset | OD: Addiction**

At Teal, Reset is spontaneous renewal. You didn't plan it. It just happened. The energy returns. The practice feels fresh again. You begin again, without effort.

This is the gift of trusting the cycle. You didn't have to force Restoration. It came on its own, because it always does.

Addiction at Teal is subtle. It's the craving for the "special" experiences. The desire to always feel connected, always feel flow. It's preference disguised as devotion.

The medicine is equanimity. You let the good states come. You let them go. You don't chase them. You trust that the path is unfolding exactly as it needs to.

Reset is natural. Addiction is grasping. Choose trust.
