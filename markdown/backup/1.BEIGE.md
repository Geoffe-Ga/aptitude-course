## What is Beige?

About the automatic, reactionary survival mode upon which civil society is a thin veneer, fantasy author Patrick Rothfuss paints the following scene. For context the narrator was a homeless orphan for years in the crime ridden city of Tarbean:

> Then someone touched me on the shoulder. I jumped fully two feet into the air and narrowly avoided falling on Simmon in the howling, scratching, biting blur that had been my only method of defense in Tarbean.

~ Patrick Rothfuss, Name of the Wind

Beige relates to the aspect of our identity that deals with making sure we have something to eat, we’re watered, and the people around us won’t slit our throats in our sleep.

It corresponds to the Root Chakra of a Whole Adept. Although each Chakra, each Stage, has its own versions of both Masculine and Feminine, I identify Beige more with the Divine Masculine.

Divine Gender, of course, has nothing to do with embodied sex or gender identity. It‘s an archetypal force that inherits its name from esoteric spirituality.

The Divine Masculine is not aggressive or dominating, but agentic. Protective. Practical.

It is the doing energy that sparks us all to life.

It is primal, in the sense of being one of our first concerns, both as a species and as growing humans.

Doing the hunting or gathering needed to feed yourself.

Doing the work of screaming for your mother’s milk at 3am.

Doing everything you have to do to protect yourself from threats.

In short, doing what needs to be done so that your nervous system can trust you, and the world, again.

In Spiral Dynamics, Beige is the pre-conceptual layer. The animal instinct. The fundamental orientation toward staying alive. In APTITUDE, we evolve it. It is the Agency Aspect of Yes-And-Ness. The “And”—that sacred sense that you can do things, and those things matter.

You may not think you need Beige. You might feel like you're already far beyond it. But let me ask you:

- Are you drinking water regularly?
- Do you move your body daily?
- Are your mornings and nights anchored with practices that restore your energy?
- Are you sleeping well?
- Are your basic financial and energetic needs met in a way that nourishes you?

If not… then welcome home. Beige has been waiting within you.

## Why Beige Matters: The Physiology of Safety and the Soul of Stability

Beige is not just a metaphorical stage of development.

It’s a somatic and physiological stage. By different names, this aspect was identified in psychology as the foundation of human development.

- In Erik Erikson’s Psychosocial Stages of Development, it is the Trust vs Mistrust phase
- In Jean Piaget’s Theory of Cognitive Development, it is the Sensorimotor phase
- In Abraham Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs, it is the Physiological and Safety Needs (the Base of the Hierarchy)
- Clare Graves is where we get the “Beige” terminology from and in Spiral Dynamics, this stage is called the SurvivalSense value meme.
- Ken Wilber’s Integral Theory refers to it as the
  Archaic-Instinctual (Fulcrum 1, Pre-Self)

… and countless other researchers have identified the existence of a basic, fundamental, and foundational component of human experience.

Please don’t memorize any of these names or the theoretical jargon that accompanies them. Just know that by focusing on your physiological needs first, you are in the company of countless wise researchers.

But not just researchers, mystics too!

In Eastern Body, Western Mind, Anodea Judith describes the root chakra—the energetic corollary to Beige—as the realm of physical security, trust, and survival. It governs our legs, feet, bones, immune system, and elimination systems. The Root is the part of us that says: “I am here.” Without a balanced root, the body cannot feel safe. And if the body doesn’t feel safe, the psyche cannot develop.
All growth from that point on becomes compensatory.

According to Judith, when the root is underdeveloped, people experience:

- Disconnection from their bodies
- Chronic anxiety or dissociation
- Poor boundaries
- Trouble finishing things
- Difficulty trusting life

They may be dreamers, intellectuals, or creatives who float in the realm of lofty ideals, mystical notions, and creative passions but who can’t seem to actualize any of their visions or plans.
Nothing can be manifested without a strong anchor in 3D reality. Those with a weak Root may spiritualize their trauma to avoid dealing with it.

In the Chakra system, people who overcompensate this way are sometimes referred to as “top-heavy.” But it’s possible to be both underdeveloped in some ways and overdeveloped in others at the same time.

Trust me, I know. From experience.

When the root is overdeveloped, people exhibit:

- Obsession with control and routine
- Fear of change
- Hoarding of resources (money, food, time)
- Compulsions around safety or cleanliness
- Hyper-focus on material security

Those with an overdeveloped root, can sometimes be pragmatic to a fault. They may resist change even when growth requires it.

They are often anxious in disguise—like a duck who seems to glide along the water, but whose webbed toes are a furious chaos of thrashing, just beneath the surface.

These conditions of being under- or overdeveloped occur due to needs going unmet in our earliest years but while healing your neglected or abused child self will help sort out these problems, another school of thought suggests “fake it till you make it.”

> “To feel brave, act as if we were brave.”

~ William James

Sometimes the best way to rewire the brain is simply by doing something different until your brain learns that you are someone new.

That might sound pat or out of reach, but this chapter offers the tools that worked for me, so take heart. Prior to developing what I call “Energy Scaffolding,” I’d tried a hundred programs for “just doing it” and nothing had stuck.

I became obsessed with Free Will and the sense that I was a Biological Machine by observing myself mindlessly playing out habits, almost like a hell of karmic loops I could never escape. If that resonates, I hope this works for you too.

Something undoubtedly will—just keep striking matches until one stays lit.

Plus, learning about a model of development has been shown to improve an individual’s ability to advance through it. Just by reading about this, you are solidifying your base. Where attention goes, energy flows.

Some of the most innovative models of development of our time are Integral Theory and Spiral Dynamics. But discovering them was more of an experience of validation than of inspiration. I had also identified the necessity of the basic agency available at this foundational level according to a system of my own: the Aspects of Adepthood Wholeness, in which it is referred to as the Agentic Aspect of Yes-And-Ness, which we’ll cover in greater detail in the next chapter, once were introduced to the “Yes.”

For now, take this seriously. Until your root is stable, your higher pursuits—intuition, compassion, insight, transcendence—are built on shaky ground. You can’t fake that kind of stability. Your nervous system knows.

> Some of you seek to fly. You see a trajectory where there is no mooring on this plane. But you must hold a mooring while in physical form. Not only to the body, but to all things which vibrate at the level of tone at which the body does.

## The Journaling Prompts of Beige

To further anchor this learning, Beige invites you into two core journaling practices. Some basic rules first:

1.  Try to write at least once a week for 20-30
    minutes.
2.  Don’t let your pen stop moving for the entire
    time—and yes, use pen not a pencil; a permanent tool helps silence the inner critic.
3.  If you misspell a word or think of a better one,
    ignore it and keep writing.
4.  Don’t reread or cross anything out.
5.  If you can’t think of what to write next, just write that: “I can think of nothing to say so I’m just vamping and it’s really dull and…” Eventually an idea will come to you if you learn to rest on the page.

This is a bit like a meditation practice where you are concentrating on the workings of your own mind rather than your breath, a mantra, a candle flame, etc. Beginning to train your attention will pay off.

Here’s the prompts:

1.  List the systemic, social, and cultural influences
    that contribute to your sense of security.

    Safety is never just personal—it’s contextual. By naming the structures that hold (or undermine) your sense of rootedness, you begin to reclaim discernment. This clarity will help you determine what to protect, what to shift, and what to release.
    
2.  “What kind of hero did I need to feel secure when I was a kid? What would it take to become that person in the next 9 months?”

    This question calls you back to your primal needs. It bypasses abstract goals and gets straight to the part of you that longs for protection, for care, for steady presence. If you can identify that hero and start showing up as them—bit by bit—you are laying spiritual rebar into your foundation.

3.  If none of these questions move you, journal about your challenges and successes working with the APTITUDE program.

    Journaling has been shown to dramatically improve quality of life, causing you to recover from traumatic life events like a break-up or a lay-off much quicker than those who do not have the habit of emptying their worries and triumphs onto the page. Journaling also has the effect of reducing rumination, improving recall, and boosting creativity.

As a final step, make sure you share the results and insights derived from your journaling with your Digital Sangha. Share. Listen. React. Engage. The Sangha is meant to be a springboard, so get to bouncing off of it!

## The Relationship to Free Will at Beige

Let’s get right to it. What is the relationship to Free Will? That’s what this whole course is about after all, isn’t it?

When an individual is at the “structure stage”—a phrase signifying a nearly universal rite of passage that we all must work through to realize our Wholeness—of Beige, there is no complex concept of “free will.” It’s rare to make it to adulthood still at Beige, and rarer to sign up for a course about Free Will when your concerns are hung up in its realm. But you can identify your past Beige behaviors and if you haven’t integrated this root stage properly, you may still recognize some of its chief concerns lingering in your current personality, either as allergies or addictions.

When we first move through Beige we operate like a Biological Machine—a bundle of reflexes, needs, cravings, and instincts. Basically, a baby. An addict deep in the territory of abuse. A victim of trauma, deprivation, or circumstance struggling to survive.

It’s not something to feel guilty about.

In fact, your first step to true Agency is to recognize whether you have either an allergy or an addiction to physical or financial security that comes at the expense of a Whole human experience.

While the material of this course teaches you to integrate the lessons of what Spiral Dynamics calls Tier 1 stages (the first six, in which the very philosophical concept of Free Will isn’t typically a primary concern) it is also important to note that if you do not also transcend them, Free Will is impossible.

That means having the discernment to figure out what pragmatically serves when it comes to our basic biological needs, and when to let go of obsessing about safety and security because you’ve done enough to ground yourself.

After all, you are safe. One of my teachers, Paul Selig, channels Guides who assert that there could be nothing safer than incarnation. After all, according to these incorporeal Guides, there are no stakes. We’ll outlive these bodies, anyway.

And I’d add: or we won’t.

Either way what happens in this life will quickly cease to matter.

But let’s not get ahead of ourselves. You’re probably partly here because you vibe on those kinds of gossamer wisps of sacred understanding—the leaves that absorb the pure, unfiltered Light of source—but first let's attend to the roots’ needs for stability in a storm.

We’re here. Now. In a body. The Guides also say it is our responsibility to care for it.

## The Grounding Protocol: 5-4-3-2-1

And when you do feel yourself slipping into that Overdose energy—when your crown chakra swells and you start chasing too many dreams at once—that’s where the first Practice of the path comes in.

Practices are meant to be a focal point for the duration of the Stage during which they are introduced and they are designed to progressively build meditation skills up from next to nothing.

Here your Practice will only take a few minutes or less, but by the end of the program, you’ll be comfortably meditating for 45 minutes at a time.

What is it?

The default Practice of Beige is called the 5-4-3-2-1 Technique, and it brings you back to Earth.

When you find yourself slipping, dissociating, or becoming a maniac, you can take a breath, center yourself and silently name:

- 5 things you can see

- 4 things you can feel

- 3 things you can hear

- 2 things you can smell

- 1 thing you can taste

I’ll introduce some alternatives as well, but these kinds of rituals begin our journey toward a daily meditation practice gently and more importantly, they reconnect us to the material world, to our body, and to our own inner awareness.

It’s the parachute Icarus needed when he started soaring too high. We’ve all been in that place where our ego seeks to eat the sun. What might be unlocked if we chose instead to return to Earth?

## The Default Habit of Beige: Take Work Seriously

To begin correcting these root-level imbalances, we have the recommendation slash option to anchor in a Habit that is essential to physical security: Taking Work Seriously.

This doesn't mean becoming a grindset-having, productivity-maxing, biohacking capitalist robot. Not unless you’re doing those things consciously, with a respect for your Wavelength and a willingness to honor the rhythms of your biology.

What it really means is restoring reverence for focused, effortful engagement with money-generating obligations. In the philosophy of Don Miguel Ruiz, author of the acclaimed Four Agreements, this is the Agreement we make with ourselves to “always do our best.”

Think on it. If you are receiving an hourly wage for your work, you are getting paid to trade away hours of your life. This is a finite resource. If you are a salaried employee that’s not too different: you are being paid to trade away your intention and attention with an agreed upon level of effort, week after week, month after month.

This can trick us into masking our personality while at work. We think we are being paid for our life energy and so we are obligated to slip into a posture of zombified compliance while we are on the clock.

This is a waste. A terrible waste.

A better attitude would be to find ways to use work as a learning experience for your human incarnation.

You’re in soul school, take the curriculum!

~ Ram Dass

Maybe you have a little free time to listen to audiobooks or self-improvement podcasts, and that could be great. Take advantage!

But maybe the way you improve yourself is more directly grounded in the work you are doing. Can you practice intentionally directing your focus while working? Can you use your time earning money to shift your habitual focus from a fixed mindset—a belief that you are born with fixed abilities and can’t change them—to the sort of growth mindset that is oriented toward constant betterment and elevation?

If you’re a Householder Mystic and not a secluded monastic, you’re going to spend a lot of time working (about a third of our lives, right?). That means it has to be harnessed as an opportunity to advance our project of becoming Whole. By bringing our whole self to work and “doing our best” to become better every day, we bake a pattern into our personality that views an orientation toward growth as a fundamental given.

Any activity, no matter how menial, can be an opportunity to deploy the mindfulness and care to make it an artisanal craft.

Thich Naht Hanh was known to say that when putting away dishes, you can make them scream with your carelessness or sing with your gentle intention.

Mopping the floor you can lazily slop water around, or you can deliberately, systematically cleanse each linoleum tile with a kind of brush stroke that acts as a banishing ritual.

As an engineer, you can phone in messy, barely functional spaghetti code, or you can program as if you are writing poetry.

When you bring attention to it, any job becomes meaningful.

### Work can be either a Job, a Career, or a Calling.

And the same occupation can be any of the three.

A janitor who sees cleaning the floors of a hospital as a necessary drudgery to feed his family has a Job.



The same janitor might shift into a mindset where he takes meticulous care, determined to prove himself to his boss so he can be promoted.
This is someone who treats his work as a step in his Career: an opportunity to build safety and security.



Meanwhile, one of that janitor’s coworkers who does the exact same work can see his contribution to the hospital as a vital service to the complex system that helps save lives. This janitor has adopted the mindset of a Calling.
Her work is meaningful. She takes pleasure and pride in bringing her complete self to every shift.

## Energy Scaffolding

If you aren’t called to stick with the default Habits, your APTITUDE journey hinges on the process of Energy Scaffolding: the sacred art of building your life one Habit at a time. Every 21 days, you’ll add a new behavior to your stack—a Habit that gives more energy back to you than it takes to engage with it. You choose habits that feed you.


It is imperative that you focus completely on a single Habit and build up slow but steady progress. You may feel painfully impatient with only adding or subtracting a single new behavior every three weeks, but after you write down everything you want your perfect life to have, multiply it by three weeks and divide by 52 to get the number of years it will take… I’m betting you’ll be surprised by how soon you can have your perfect life.

And it will definitely be sooner than you’d get that life if you were stuck stutter-starting as I’m sure you have been for the last long while. As Tim Ferris says, many of us are like the donkey stuck between a bale of hay and a trough of water, both hungry and thirsty but unable to decide which need to sate first. A donkey that doesn’t realize it can do one for a bit and then come back for the other, partially recharged, might die of both starvation and dehydration, while indecisive in the middle.

Still we have to prioritize. If you’re like me you’ve probably started and abandoned a million plans for self-improvement.
This might not even be the first time that you’ve adopted a plan to intentionally tweak your daily Habit stack. But Energy Scaffolding is different.

You’ll use the energy from the last habit to fuel the next. Slowly. Patiently. Like building a bonfire one twig at a time.

This is how we reclaim our doing. This is how we begin to trust ourselves. This is how we prove to our nervous systems that we are no longer at the mercy of our moods or cravings. We are choosing, deliberately, to show up.

## How to Perform Energy Scaffolding

Step 1: Open a spreadsheet. Make four columns:
Habit, Energy Invested, Energy Returned, and Net Energy.

Step 2: Brainstorm like your hair is on fire. Write down every habit you’ve ever thought about starting (or stopping). No wrong answers. This is your dream buffet. You can have it all. I was overwhelmed when I wrote down everything that I wanted to quit—smoking, drinking, caffeine, doomscrolling—and everything I wanted to start—cold showers, vitamins, water, exercise, journaling, meditation, breathwork, Metta. But a year and a half later, every single one of those things is part of my life. Be honest. Be hopeful.
Be extra. You really can have it all—just not all at once.

Step 3: Energy Invested. Rate each habit from -10 to +10. A -10 means the habit would actually give you energy just to think about doing it. A +10 means it’s so hard to get yourself to do it that you might cry just imagining it. Trust your gut. You’re rating how much energy it takes to begin.

Step 4: Now hide that column. This next part works best when your brain isn’t trying to cheat.

Step 5: Energy Returned. Same scale, -10 to +10.
Except this time, rate how you feel afterward. A +10 means you’re floating, glowing, kissing babies. A -10 means the habit makes you feel like a husk. Again: honesty is sacred here.

Step 6: Unhide the first column and calculate Net Energy = Energy Returned - Energy Invested for each row. You’re now looking at a live blueprint of your energy ecosystem.

Step 7: Sort the spreadsheet. First by Net Energy (highest first), then by Energy Invested (lowest first), then Energy Returned (again, highest first). Boom. You’ve just found what behavior researcher Charles Duhig calls your “Keystone Habits”—the habits that give the most and cost the least. These are your low-hanging fruit and the perfect place to start.

Step 8: Create a new column: Start Date. Use the date you’re beginning the APTITUDE journey as the top row. Add 21 days to each row below. This is the pacing mechanism that makes everything work. Trust the rhythm.

Step 9: Add three more columns: Low Grit Goal, Clear Goal, Stretch Goal. Use the SMART method here:

- Specific: What exactly will
  you do? (e.g. Drink water)
- Measurable: How will you
  track it? (e.g. Ounces per day)
- Achievable: Is it realistic? “A” should also
  stand, however gently, for Ambitious.
- Relevant: Does it serve your
  Aspirational Identity? (More below.)
- Time-bound: What’s the time
  commitment? (This is kind of a given. You should be shooting for being fully integrated with each habit by the 21st morning).

Set three levels for each habit:

- Low Grit: So easy you’d be embarrassed not to do it.
  (Ex: “Make a special effort to drink 8oz of water, 3x per day.”)
- Clear Goal: Just right. Balanced and reasonable. (Ex:
  “Drink 8x8oz of water per day.”)
- Stretch Goal: The fantasy version. (Ex: “ONE. GALLON.
  DAILY.”)

Step 10: Aspirational Identity. Look at your top 10 habits. Ask: Who does this every day? What kind of archetype is slowly emerging here? Is it the Whole Adept? A Conscious Biohacker? A Loving Partner? A Pinterest Witch-Meets-Urban Monk hybrid? Create a name or symbol that resonates. It can even be mythical or fictional character. Are you channeling the elephant god Ganesh, as biohacker and angel investor Tim Ferris does? Or maybe the dense data packet you’re choosing is more visual, like a pinterest vision board. Maybe the Aspirational Identity you wish to embody is Neo from the Matrix.

Choose something resonant. This is who you're becoming. Every time you show up for a habit, whisper to yourself: “That’s right. I AM \[insert badass aspirational self here\].”

## The Four Gates of Habit Alchemy

An essential pocket summary of James Clears’ philosophy from Atomic Habits.

Let’s say you’ve just picked a habit—maybe it’s meditation, or taking your vitamins, or journaling instead of screaming into the void. Great.
Now what? How do you actually do the thing?

Number one? It’s not about brute force. The people with the most agency don’t actually flex their willpower muscles much. Instead, they make willpower their last resort and set up their lives to make following through on their aspirations the path of least resistance.

Did you know that willpower is literally a finite resource? It’s why Steve Jobs and Mitch Horowitz pared down their wardrobes to an essential look. Every decision you make depletes a little of your allotted willpower for the day.

It’s why it’s harder to refuse dessert after a busy shift at work than it is to stick to your diet first thing in the morning. The more tired we are the more prone we are to giving in.

“Deplete me not into temptation” as one scientific paper on agency puts it.

So how do you set up your day to use the least willpower possible to make changes?

Habit expert James Clear identified four “Laws”—and each one corresponds to a phase in the life of a habit.

### 1. Make it Obvious (Cue)

Want to start a habit? Make the cue unmissable. Want to quit one? Hide the cue—out of sight, out of mind. If your meditation cushion is buried under laundry, your odds of using it just dropped to near zero. But if it’s on your bed, or lit by a candle, or placed exactly where you stumble every morning—now it’s obvious.

If you want to eat more fruit, leave the apples on the counter, not in the cupboard. If you want to quit drinking, don’t go to parties where you have to battle yourself to grab a Diet Coke instead of a free Pabst every trip you take to the cooler.

Pro tip: Stack it. “After I brush my teeth, I’ll do 3 minutes of breathwork.” The Cue is something obvious: a habit you’ve already mastered. That’s habit piggybacking. No new routines—just new passengers.

2. Make it Attractive
(Craving)

We’ve all got that part that sighs: “Ooooh, yeah. I want that.” It’s a pressure in the chest, like the tension before a sneeze. All habits are driven by desire. Even, and in many cases especially, the bad ones. So how do you lure your nervous system toward the good stuff?

Bundle your habits with dopamine. Turn breathwork into a treat by doing it with music. Pair cold showers with your favorite playlist. Light incense when you journal. Drink ceremonial cacao before meditating like you’re a Mayan priest. Whatever makes your inner animal go “Yes, please.”

Pro tip: Use the temptation bundling trick: Only let yourself do something indulgent after the habit. Like “I can scroll TikTok for a bit, but only after I work out.” The default mode of rapid cognitive chaos that meditators call “monkey mind” can be surprisingly obedient with the right incentives.

### 3. Make it Easy (Response)

Think about your finite willpower. It’s a muscle you can build like any other, but it will always be true that if a habit requires your soul to metaphorically crest Mount Everest every time you try it… you won’t.

Lower the friction. Make the barrier to entry laughably small. Want to become a yoga wizard? Start by rolling out the mat. That’s it. Want to become a writer? Commit to one sentence a day.
Want to get back to running? Try just going to sleep in your workout clothes and stepping from bed right into your running shoes. Once you start, you’ll probably keep going—you’ll probably feel a bit silly if you don’t—but the key is to make starting effortless.

Pro tip: Use the 2-minute rule: Make the first step so quick—make it take under two minutes—and easy it feels ridiculous not to follow through.

### 4. Make it Satisfying (Reward)

Beige teaches that we are not solely rational beings.
We are also dopamine-seeking, feedback-addicted mammals who need a little gold star to keep going.

Celebrate every win. Smile. Fist pump. Say your Aspirational Identity out loud: “That’s right. I AM the IT Monk.” Track your streak. Give yourself stickers. Make the end of your habit feel like a mini-victory every single time.

The surest way I’ve found to associate pleasure with an activity is to thank yourself for following through on it like you’re treat-training a Golden Retriever. Take a big luxuriant sigh, like you’re sinking into a hot tub, and say to yourself, “Whoosa good human?!”

When you congratulate yourself, note any present wholesome or pleasant sensations, like a gentle breeze, a comfortable seat, or a feeling of accomplishment. Once you’ve identified a few, make an intentional effort to associate them with the task you just succeeded at. Eventually your brain will generate those feel-good chemicals automatically whenever you complete your chosen task.

Pro tip: Create a visual tracker. A calendar, a spreadsheet, an app, a string of beads on your altar—something that gives you the joy of watching progress accumulate. Momentum is a sacred drug; why not get addicted to something that improves your life?

Now flip it: Want to break a habit?

- Make it invisible.
- Make it unattractive.
- Make it hard.
- Make it unsatisfying.

Hide your vape. Leave your phone in the car. Add friction between you and your impulse. Reward yourself for saying no. If that sounds manipulative, well… it is. But you're the one being manipulated. You’re just reclaiming the steering wheel from your unconscious autopilot.

That’s what this whole thing is about: moving from unconscious reactivity to conscious ritual.

Burning emergency fuel to keep my lanterns lit? That’s the overdose.

Steadiness, by contrast, is the skill of recognizing that I am in the beginning moments of the Withdrawal Phase and deliberately taking stock of what I am committed to, and what can be jettisoned.

Maybe I was doing a little extra while riding the Peak.

To gracefully, equanimously let go of everything else, no matter how much I enjoyed having those exciting extras in my life.

## The 5-4-3-2-1 Technique (Your Root Chakra Reset)

When your crown chakra starts inflating and you feel your feet leaving the ground—when your to-do list gets wild and your dreams start tasting like mania—pause and drop into this:

5 things you can SEE (Look around. Call them out. Ground into the room.)

4 things you can FEEL (Your feet on the ground. The breath in your nose. The texture of your shirt. Your heartbeat.)

3 things you can HEAR (Electronics humming, a bird tittering, your own breath wooshing.)

2 things you can SMELL (Your tea, your shirt, your home.)

1 thing you can TASTE (A sip of water. A breath of air. A grounding reminder that you are here.)

This technique resets your nervous system. It plugs your awareness back into your body. It plants you in the soil of the present moment.

## Alternative Practice Options

Although the 5-4-3-2-1 technique is like a great bit of Pratyahara (or yogic “turning inward”), many people find it hard to remember the order or keep the numbers straight.

That extra bit of thinking is part of what grounds you, and employing patience toward it will definitely lower your activation level before you shoot into space, but as with all the Stages, I’ll offer some alternative Practices so you can choose what suits you best.

- Square - Circle - Triangle
  x5: Find things that are those shapes in the room you’re in. Go in a cycle 5 times.
- Colors of the Rainbow x 3:
  Silently name things in the room you’re in that are the colors of the rainbow. Start with red and go in order three times.
- Clench/Relax body scan: Begin
  with your toes, clench on inhale, relax on exhale. Work your way up your body (and back down if you like). Works especially well in savasana.
- Stand Barefoot Outdoors: Take
  two to three minutes to touch some grass! Stand still with your arms at your sides. Focus on the feeling of your weight being securely held by the Earth as it presses up into the souls and balls of your feet.
  Remember that no matter where you are, the Earth’s core is always directly beneath you. Tap into it.

## A Note on Beige Practice

Play around with different techniques to find the one that suits you. Figure out what brings you back into your body.

One thing to bear in mind, however, is that whichever you choose should take no more than 1-3 minutes. This is the first Stage of APTITUDE, and the first Practice is meant to be the world’s gentlest introduction to meditation. Yes, you should be grounding yourself as often as is required. And keep track somewhere, even if it's just a tally mark in a bullet journal, a note on your phone or a spreadsheet you return to each day.

Whatever you measure is what your mind recognizes as something that matters.

Over the course of APTITUDE, we’ll be kicking it up a notch every three weeks. Maybe you’ll find that the Practice of Grounding is something you want to keep around, but it isn’t necessary to stack Practices the same way we are shooting to stack Habits.

Eventually we will get to a point, increasing by just a few minutes every few weeks, where you are meditating solidly for 45 minutes a day—a healthy householder minimum that brings Bliss, Awakening, and Wholeness within reach.

For now, start small. 1-3 minutes. But do it as often as necessary.
