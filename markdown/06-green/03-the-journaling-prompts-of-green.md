## The Journaling Prompts of Green

Green's journaling is different. It's not strategic (like Orange) or reflective (like Purple). It's *alchemical*. You're using the page to metabolize what the body can't hold alone. To externalize the shadow so you can see it clearly. To transmute pain into insight.

As always: pen, not pencil. Timer set. Hand moving. No crossing out. Let the unconscious speak.

**Prompt 1: Write About a Memory Associated With Each Shadow Element (At least 4x per week)**

Start by making your Shadow List (see Prompt 2 below). Then, for each element on that list—each behavior, characteristic, or identity you've disowned—write about a specific memory where you first learned that this part of you was unacceptable.

Go back to the moment. Describe it in detail. What were you doing? Who was there? What did they say? What did you feel in your body?

Don't analyze it yet. Just *be* with it. Let the memory unfold on the page like a film. Feel it fully.

Then—and this is the alchemical part—reframe the shadow element as Light. Ask: What gift was hiding in this behavior? What need was I trying to meet? What would it look like to honor this part of myself *without* the shame?

For example: If your Shadow element is "anger," write about the first time you were punished for being angry. Then reframe: "My anger was trying to protect my boundaries. It was telling me something important. If I could honor that energy without the violence, what would it become? Clarity. Advocacy. Sacred rage."

This is shadow work. Not eliminating the dark, but *illuminating* it. Seeing it clearly enough to integrate it.

**Prompt 2: Make a List of Behaviors, Characteristics, and Identities That Comprise Your Shadow (At least 4x per week)**

Your Shadow is everything you've disowned. Every quality you've been told is "bad" or "wrong" or "too much" or "not enough." Every part of yourself you've hidden because it didn't fit the image of who you're supposed to be.

Make the list. Be specific. Go deeper than "anger" or "sadness." Get granular.

Examples:
- My tendency to withdraw when I'm overwhelmed
- The part of me that wants to be seen as special
- My jealousy when someone I love gives attention to someone else
- The voice that says I'm lazy
- My fear of being ordinary
- The part of me that secretly enjoys gossip
- My rage at injustice
- My need to control outcomes
- The part of me that's terrified of failure
- My desire to be taken care of

These aren't "bad" things. They're *human* things. But somewhere along the way, you learned that they were unacceptable. So you buried them. And now they're running your life from the basement.

The first step to reclaiming them is naming them. Write them all down. Don't judge. Just witness.

**Prompt 3: Identify the Shadow Characteristics That Project Systemic Problems (At least 4x per week)**

This is where personal shadow work meets collective responsibility. Return to your list of Problems from Orange. Then ask: Which of my Shadow elements *contribute* to these problems?

For example:
- Problem: "People don't know how to have conflict without destroying relationships."
- Shadow element: "I avoid confrontation because I'm terrified of being disliked."
- Connection: My personal avoidance pattern reinforces a culture where we can't speak honestly to each other. My shadow is perpetuating the system I claim to oppose.

Or:
- Problem: "Climate anxiety is paralyzing instead of mobilizing."
- Shadow element: "I feel helpless and overwhelmed by big problems."
- Connection: My internalized helplessness is exactly what keeps me (and others) stuck. If I could reclaim my agency, I could contribute to solutions instead of collapsing into despair.

This isn't about blame. It's about *response-ability*. You're recognizing that your inner work *is* outer work. That healing your shadow is how you stop perpetuating the patterns you want to change in the world.

Write about these connections. Let yourself see how personal and systemic are always interwoven. This is Green's gift: the understanding that there is no "inner" and "outer." It's all one field.

**Example:**

Let's say your Shadow List includes: "My tendency to stay silent when I disagree" and "My fear of being seen as difficult."

And your Problem List includes: "So many organizations claim to value diversity but silence dissenting voices."

The connection: *Your personal pattern of silencing yourself is a microcosm of the systemic pattern you oppose. Every time you stay quiet to keep the peace, you're reinforcing the culture of conflict-avoidance that allows bad ideas to go unchallenged.*

This doesn't mean you're responsible for the whole system. But it does mean your healing is *part* of the solution. When you reclaim your voice—when you learn to speak truth even when it's uncomfortable—you're not just healing yourself. You're modeling a different possibility for everyone around you.

**Prompt 4: Share and Discuss Shadow Work With Your Digital Sangha**

Shadow work is not meant to be done alone. When you share what you're discovering—when you let others witness your process—the shame loses its grip. You realize: *Oh. I'm not the only one. We're all carrying this.*

Share a few of the relationships you've identified between your Shadow and the Problems you care about. Invite others to reflect theirs. Create space for vulnerability. This is how community becomes medicine.

Here's why sharing matters:

**1. Shame Dissolves in Witnessing**

Shame thrives in secrecy. It tells you, "If anyone knew this about you, they'd reject you." But when you speak your shadow out loud and someone responds with, "Me too. I struggle with that exact thing"—the shame loses its power. You realize you're not uniquely broken. You're just human.

**2. Patterns Become Visible**

When multiple people share their shadow work, you start to see collective patterns. "Oh, we're all afraid of being too much. We've all learned to make ourselves small." This isn't just personal—it's *cultural*. And recognizing that helps you see the bigger picture: this isn't your fault. You inherited this wound. And now you're choosing to heal it.

**3. Accountability Without Shame**

When you share a pattern you're working on, the community can gently reflect it back to you when they see it showing up. Not to shame you, but to help you stay conscious. "Hey, I noticed you apologized three times in that message. Is that the people-pleasing pattern you've been exploring?" This kind of loving feedback is invaluable.

**4. Collective Healing Accelerates Individual Healing**

When you witness someone else do brave shadow work, it gives you permission to go deeper in yours. When you see someone integrate a shadow element and become more whole, you believe it's possible for you too. Community doesn't just hold you—it *inspires* you.

**How to Share Skillfully**

- **Ask for consent.** Don't trauma-dump. Say, "I'm working through something related to my shadow. Is anyone available to witness?" Let people opt in.

- **Be specific.** Don't just say, "I'm struggling." Name the pattern. Describe the shadow element. Share the connection you're seeing. Specificity creates intimacy.

- **Don't perform vulnerability.** Share to connect, not to be seen as brave or deep. The community can tell the difference.

- **Reciprocate.** If someone shares, witness them. Reflect what you heard. Don't try to fix them. Just say, "I see you. Thank you for trusting us with this."

This is Green's relational practice. Not just shadow work in isolation, but shadow work in community. Where the healing is multiplied.
