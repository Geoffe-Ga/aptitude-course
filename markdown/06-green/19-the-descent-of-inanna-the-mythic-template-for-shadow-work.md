## The Descent of Inanna: The Mythic Template for Shadow Work

Let me tell you the oldest recorded myth about shadow work. It's Sumerian. Four thousand years old. And it maps Green's journey with eerie precision.

Inanna was the Queen of Heaven. Goddess of love, war, fertility, and power. She had everything—beauty, status, divine authority. But her sister, Ereshkigal, ruled the underworld. The land of the dead. The shadow realm. And Inanna, for reasons the myth never fully explains, decided she needed to descend.

She dressed in all her regalia—crown, necklace, breastplate, robes—and approached the first gate of the underworld. The gatekeeper demanded she remove one item to pass. So she did. At the second gate, another item. By the seventh gate, she stood naked and powerless.

When she finally reached Ereshkigal, her sister killed her. Hung her corpse on a hook. Left her to rot.

Three days later, her loyal servant sent emissaries to plead for her return. But Ereshkigal would only release Inanna if someone took her place. Inanna emerged from the underworld—but she was no longer the same. She had died and been reborn. She had met her shadow and been devoured by it. And only then could she become whole.

This is Green's template. This is the map.
