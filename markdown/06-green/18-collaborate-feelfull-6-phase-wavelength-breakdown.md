## "COLLABORATE (Feel)"—Full 6-Phase Wavelength Breakdown

Now we descend into Green's full Wavelength—the rhythm of deep relational work. This is slower than Orange, softer than Red, more embodied than anything you've encountered so far. Each phase asks you to *feel* your way through, trusting the intelligence of emotion and body.

### 1. RISING

**Rx: Connection | OD: Oversharing**

Rising in Green is about reaching out. About moving toward intimacy. In its medicine form, this is **Connection**—the act of showing up authentically and letting yourself be seen.

Connection means you share what's true for you without needing the other person to fix it or validate it. You're vulnerable, but boundaried. You're open, but not desperate. You're saying, "This is where I am. I trust you enough to show you."

This is the energy of a good friendship deepening. Of a circle where people start to share beneath the surface. Of a therapeutic relationship where trust is building. You're letting someone in—not all the way, but enough to feel the warmth of real contact.

But when the need for connection becomes compulsive—when you're so starved for intimacy that you grasp at it—Rising becomes **Oversharing**.

Oversharing is vomiting your feelings onto someone who didn't consent to hold them. It's trauma-dumping in the first conversation. It's mistaking intensity for intimacy. It's using vulnerability as a way to hook someone's attention, not as a genuine offer of connection.

You've probably experienced this: the person who tells you their whole life story within five minutes of meeting. Who shares every detail of their trauma before you've even learned their last name. It feels overwhelming. Invasive. Like they're asking you to be their therapist without your permission.

Oversharing isn't intimacy. It's a boundary violation dressed up as authenticity.

The difference is *consent* and *pacing*. Connection happens when both people are ready for it. Oversharing happens when one person is so desperate to be seen that they forget to check if the other person is actually available.

When you notice yourself slipping into Oversharing, slow down. Take a breath. Ask yourself: Am I sharing to connect, or to discharge? Is this person actually available for this level of depth, or am I forcing it?

Connection is a dance. Oversharing is a monologue. Learn to feel the difference.

### 2. PEAKING

**Rx: Belonging | OD: Megalomania**

This is the summit of Green's Wavelength—the moment when you feel *held* in authentic community. When you've found your people. When the circle is strong and you know, bone-deep, that you belong.

In its medicine form, this is **Belonging**—the experience of being fully yourself and fully accepted. Not despite your weirdness, but *because* of it. This is the sangha at its best. The chosen family. The space where you don't have to perform.

Belonging is one of the most healing experiences a human can have. It rewires the nervous system. It tells the body: *You're safe here. You can rest.* It's the antidote to the isolation that modern life breeds.

When you're in Belonging, you feel grounded in the We. You trust that you can show up messy and still be loved. You know that your presence matters. That you're not just tolerated—you're *cherished*.

This is Green at its peak. This is why the stage exists. To teach you that Wholeness is not a solo project.

But—and this is crucial—Belonging has a shadow. When the feeling of being special within the group becomes intoxicating, Peaking can curdle into **Megalomania**.

Megalomania at Green looks like: believing your community is the only enlightened one. Treating your circle like a cult. Developing an "us vs. them" mentality where anyone outside the group is "unconscious" or "toxic." It's the flip side of belonging—exclusion disguised as discernment.

You see this in spiritual communities all the time. The group that started as a healing circle becomes a closed system. The shared language becomes a code that marks insiders and outsiders. The vulnerability that once felt liberating starts to feel coercive—like you're only accepted if you perform your healing in the "right" way.

Megalomania is what happens when Belonging becomes attachment. When you mistake the container for the contents. When you start to believe that *this* group is the only place you can be whole.

The antidote is humility. Remember: this community is *a* space of belonging, not *the* space. You can be held here *and* be held elsewhere. Your Wholeness doesn't depend on this circle. It's just one expression of it.

When you can hold Belonging without grasping, without making it an identity, you've unlocked Green's deepest gift. You've learned to receive love without collapsing into codependence.

### 3. WITHDRAWAL

**Rx: Retirement | OD: Social Anxiety**

Every connection reaches a point where you need to step back. Where the intimacy gets too much, the group too loud, the demands too heavy. Withdrawal is that moment. And in its medicine form, it's **Retirement**—the conscious choice to pull back and replenish.

Retirement is not abandonment. It's *self-care*. It's recognizing that you need space to metabolize what you've experienced in the group. That you can't be in the We all the time without losing the I. That sometimes, the most loving thing you can do is say, "I need to be alone for a while."

This is the introvert's wisdom. The artist's retreat. The meditator's hermitage. You're not rejecting connection—you're honoring your need for solitude so you can return to connection refreshed.

Retirement looks like leaving the party early because you're overstimulated. Declining an invitation because you need a quiet weekend. Taking a break from the group chat. It's boundaried, clear, and guilt-free.

But when Withdrawal is driven by fear instead of need, it becomes **Social Anxiety**.

Social Anxiety is the terror of being seen. The belief that if you show up, you'll be judged, rejected, exposed as a fraud. It's the impulse to isolate not because you need rest, but because you're convinced you don't belong.

This is the shadow of Green's sensitivity. You're so aware of the relational field that you can feel every shift, every tension, every moment of misattunement. And instead of trusting that you can navigate it, you retreat. You ghost. You disappear.

Social Anxiety masquerades as self-protection, but it's actually self-abandonment. It's the part of you that says, *I'm too much / not enough / broken / unlovable*, and then removes yourself from the situation before anyone can confirm it.

The difference between Retirement and Social Anxiety is *agency*. Retirement is a choice. Social Anxiety is a compulsion.

When you notice Social Anxiety creeping in, ask: Am I withdrawing because I genuinely need rest, or because I'm afraid? If it's fear, can I stay a little longer? Can I reach out to one person and say, "I'm feeling anxious—can we talk?"

The Self-Care Strategies for Withdrawal include spending time in crowded places (paradoxically, to desensitize the fear of being seen), the "I Belong Here" ritual (a somatic practice of grounding in public), and clairsentient work with loved ones (re-attuning to safe connection).

Retirement is medicine. Social Anxiety is poison. Both live in Withdrawal. Your work is to discern which one you're in.

### 4. DIMINISHING

**Rx: Unwinding | OD: Alienation**

The energy continues to drop. You're in the downslope now, and in Green's Wavelength, that means the relational field is loosening. In its medicine form, this is **Unwinding**—the natural release of tension after deep connection.

Unwinding is what happens after a powerful conversation. After a circle where everyone went deep. After a weekend with friends. You feel the intensity starting to ease. The emotional charge dissipating. The nervous system settling back into baseline.

This is healthy. This is the body completing the cycle. You've been activated by connection, and now you're integrating it. You're letting the insights land. Letting the feelings metabolize. Unwinding is rest at the relational level.

It looks like quiet time after a big gathering. Journaling to process what came up. A long walk alone. A gentle yoga class. You're not pushing anything away—you're just letting it settle.

But when Unwinding is interrupted by fear or shame, it becomes **Alienation**.

Alienation is the belief that no one actually understands you. That you're fundamentally alone. That the connection you felt was an illusion, and now the truth is revealing itself: you don't belong.

This is the voice of the wounded child who learned early that connection is temporary. That people leave. That if you let yourself need someone, they'll disappoint you.

Alienation looks like: ghosting after a vulnerable conversation because you're convinced you shared too much. Isolating because you're certain no one actually cares. Interpreting normal distance as rejection.

It's the same pattern as Social Anxiety, but deeper. Social Anxiety fears being seen. Alienation believes you *have* been seen—and found lacking.

This is Green's core wound. The fear that authentic connection is impossible. That you're too broken, too much, too weird to truly belong.

The antidote is *reality-testing*. Reach out. Check your assumptions. Ask, "Hey, I'm feeling disconnected—are we okay?" Nine times out of ten, the other person has no idea you were spiraling. They were just busy. Or resting. Or dealing with their own stuff.

The Self-Care Strategies for Diminishing include long drives, hot beverages, walking, journaling, and cleaning (physical acts that help discharge the emotional fog). These practices help you stay present with the descent without collapsing into the story that you're alone.

Unwinding is natural. Alienation is a story. Learn to tell the difference.

### 5. BOTTOMING OUT

**Rx: Repose | OD: Isolation**

This is the lowest point. The place where all the relational energy has drained away and you're left with… yourself. In its medicine form, this is **Repose**—the deep rest that comes from complete stillness.

Repose is sacred solitude. It's the hermit's cave. The silent retreat. The moment when you stop reaching for connection and simply *are*. Not because you're avoiding anyone, but because you've found the ground of your own being and it's enough.

This is the gift that Bottoming Out offers: the reminder that you are whole on your own. That connection is beautiful, but it's not what makes you real. That you can be alone without being lonely.

Repose is when you curl up with a book and feel completely content. When you spend a weekend in silence and it feels like coming home. When you meditate and the aloneness is not empty—it's *full*.

This is the integration of Green's lesson: you need others *and* you need yourself. Both are true. And Repose is where you remember the second half.

But when Bottoming Out is driven by despair, it becomes **Isolation**.

Isolation is the belief that you *have* to be alone because no one wants you. That solitude is not a choice—it's a sentence. That you've been exiled from connection and you deserve it.

Isolation looks like: not answering texts because you're convinced no one actually wants to hear from you. Declining invitations and then feeling bitter that you're alone. Convincing yourself that you're better off without people, while secretly aching for contact.

This is the wound of abandonment fully activated. And it's *hell*.

The difference between Repose and Isolation is whether you're *choosing* to be alone or whether you believe it's the only option. Repose is empowered. Isolation is defeat.

When you're in Isolation, the hardest—and most necessary—thing to do is reach out. Not to demand connection, but to offer it. Text a friend just to say hi. Join a group. Show up to something, even if you don't feel like it. Let someone know you're struggling.

The Self-Care Strategies for Bottoming Out are gentle: readjust your posture, get comfortable, drink water, listen to music, do Lion's Breath (a fierce exhale that releases stagnant energy). These are acts of self-compassion that say, *I'm still here. I'm still with you.*

Repose is rest. Isolation is exile. Both live at the bottom. Your work is to choose rest.

### 6. RESTORATION

**Rx: Vulnerability | OD: Bitterness**

The energy begins to return. The cycle is turning upward. And at Restoration, Green offers you a choice: How will you re-enter connection?

In its medicine form, Restoration is **Vulnerability**—the willingness to show up again, softer and more open than before.

Vulnerability is what happens after you've descended, met your shadow, felt your loneliness, and survived. You come back to the circle and you say, "I went down into the dark. Here's what I found." You don't pretend you were fine. You don't hide what you learned. You bring it forward as an offering.

This is the courage of the wounded healer. The person who's been broken and is willing to share the cracks. The one who doesn't need to be perfect to be loved.

Vulnerability at Restoration looks like apologizing after you've withdrawn. Asking for reconnection after distance. Sharing that you've been struggling and you're ready to be held again. It's humble. It's real. It's *alive*.

When you can return with Vulnerability, the connection deepens. Because now people know they can trust you. Not because you're invulnerable, but because you're *honest*. You've been to the bottom and you came back. That's intimacy.

But when Restoration is poisoned by resentment, it becomes **Bitterness**.

Bitterness is the belief that you were abandoned during your descent. That you reached out and no one came. That you've been wounded by the people you trusted and now they want you to pretend it's fine. So you come back—but you come back *armored*. Cynical. Closed.

Bitterness looks like: returning to the group but staying defensive. Making passive-aggressive comments about how "no one checked on me." Punishing people for perceived neglect. Holding grudges.

This is the shadow of Vulnerability. It's the part of you that *wanted* to be rescued during Bottoming Out and feels betrayed that it didn't happen. So now you're making everyone pay.

But here's the truth: most of the time, people didn't abandon you. They just didn't know you needed them. Or they were dealing with their own stuff. Or they assumed you wanted space.

Bitterness assumes malice where there's usually just… life.

The antidote is to *communicate*. Instead of punishing people for not reading your mind, tell them what you needed. "I was really struggling last week and I could have used support. Can I reach out next time?" That's Vulnerability. It's honest, not accusatory.

The Self-Care Strategies for Restoration include Kirtan (devotional chanting to reopen the heart), taking a bath (cleansing the emotional residue), getting sunshine (reconnecting with vitality), somatic meditation, biking, jogging, dancing—anything that helps you move the stuck energy and return to aliveness.

Vulnerability is courage. Bitterness is defense. Both live in Restoration. Choose courage.
