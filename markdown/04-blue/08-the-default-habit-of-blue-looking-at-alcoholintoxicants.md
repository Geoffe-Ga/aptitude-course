## The Default Habit of Blue: Looking at Alcohol/Intoxicants

Every stage has a default habit—a behavior that either supports or undermines the work. For Blue, the habit is: **Looking at Alcohol/Intoxicants**.

Not drinking. Not using. *Looking*.

This might seem strange, but it's precise. Blue is the stage where we're most vulnerable to numbing. To self-medication. To using substances (or behaviors—scroll, shop, binge, work) to escape the discomfort of our relational lives.

Why? Because Blue is *emotionally intense*. You're learning to feel your feelings, to stay present in your relationships, to honor your commitments even when they're hard. And all of that can be overwhelming. The guilt, the resentment, the loneliness, the longing, the fear of not being enough—it's a lot.

So we reach for the thing that makes it stop. The drink that softens the edges. The scroll that distracts. The substance that lets us *not feel* for a while.

And here's the thing: sometimes that's okay. APTITUDE is not puritanical. We're not here to shame you for having a glass of wine or smoking a joint. But we *are* here to help you develop awareness around *why* you're reaching for it.

The habit of Blue is **Looking**—which means *pausing before you use*. Asking yourself:
- Why am I reaching for this right now?
- What am I trying not to feel?
- Is there another way I could meet this need?

Sometimes the answer will be, "I'm tired and I want to relax, and a beer sounds nice." Great. Enjoy it.

But sometimes the answer will be, "I'm avoiding a hard conversation with my partner," or "I feel like I failed today and I don't want to sit with the shame," or "I'm so lonely and this is the only comfort I have."

And *that's* the medicine. That's the awareness. Because once you see the pattern, you can make a choice. Maybe you still drink. But you do it *consciously*. You know what you're using it for. And that knowledge gives you power.

Or maybe you pause. And instead of numbing, you *feel*. You call a friend. You journal. You cry. You do the Metta practice. You let the discomfort move through you instead of drowning it.

This is the work of Blue: learning to stay present with your emotional life instead of escaping it. And the first step is just *looking*. Noticing. Seeing the pattern clearly.

If you find that you *can't* pause—that the urge is too strong, that the substance has you—then it might be time to ask for help. Therapy. A 12-step program. A recovery community. There's no shame in that. Blue is about belonging, and sometimes the most powerful thing you can do is reach out and say, "I can't do this alone."

But for most of us, the habit of Looking is enough. It's a gentle intervention. A moment of choice. A chance to ask: *Is this what I really want? Or is this just what I'm used to?*

Start there. See what happens.
